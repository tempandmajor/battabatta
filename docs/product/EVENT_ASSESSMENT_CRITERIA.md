# Event Assessment Criteria

This framework is for assessing existing events in an admin inventory such as `admin/quborlyevents`. The current Battarbox repository does not contain that inventory, but this rubric can be applied to each event record once the event data source is available.

Operational companion files:

- `docs/product/event-assessment-rubric.json`: machine-readable scoring criteria, categories, roles, required fields, and statuses.
- `docs/product/event-inventory-template.csv`: CSV shape for exporting/importing event records before assessment.
- `docs/product/EVENT_ASSESSMENT_RUNBOOK.md`: step-by-step event assessment workflow.

## Research Base

The rubric combines current event-industry guidance and observed patterns from successful events, conferences, festivals, performances, competitions, and TV-scale talent formats:

- Event success is measured with clearly defined goals, attendance/conversion, attendee satisfaction, NPS, engagement, sponsor/exhibitor value, revenue, and post-event feedback loops. Sources: Cvent, RainFocus, Bear Analytics, MAP D Events.
- Sustainability and social responsibility should be assessed against recognized event standards where possible. Source: Events Industry Council Sustainable Event Standards.
- Accessibility and ticketing must be built in, not treated as an add-on. Sources: ADA.gov ticket-sales guidance, ADA National Network ticketing guidance, ADA Standards for assembly areas.
- Large gatherings require crowd, emergency, ingress/egress, security, and staffing plans. Sources: U.S. DOJ COPS major special events guide, OSHA crowd-management guidance, Seattle special-event safety guidance, ANSI ES1.9 crowd-management standard.
- Economic value should consider direct attendee spending, local business effects, tax/venue impacts, and repeatable destination value. Sources: Destinations International Event Impact Calculator, MSU community event impact guidance, IMPLAN community-event impact analysis.
- Talent competitions such as American Idol succeed through a clear eligibility funnel, credible judging, public voting or transparent advancement rules, strong contestant storytelling, weekly stakes, and a winner outcome with career value. Sources: ABC American Idol auditions/voting pages and ABC's American Idol return/history coverage.

## Scoring Model

Score each event from 0 to 100. Do not publish or promote events below 70 without remediation.

| Area | Weight | What Good Looks Like |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Audience and positioning | 12 | Specific audience, clear promise, differentiated from similar events, realistic demand evidence. |
| Program and experience | 16 | Strong run-of-show, compelling talent/content, emotional arc, clear attendee journey, repeatable signature moments. |
| Operational readiness | 14 | Venue fit, permits, staffing, vendor plan, weather/contingency plan, crowd-flow plan, incident response. |
| Safety and accessibility | 12 | ADA-aware ticketing/seating, accessible routes/restrooms, security plan, emergency communications, medical support, harassment policy. |
| Business case | 16 | Credible revenue, cost model, break-even math, sponsor value, pricing logic, local economic rationale, downside plan. |
| Marketing and community | 10 | Channel plan, partnership strategy, social proof, creator/community fit, conversion funnel, post-event retention. |
| Trust and realism | 8 | Real venue/date/time, named organizer, coherent copy, believable capacity, no generic filler, verifiable logistics. |
| Measurement plan | 6 | KPIs, pre/post surveys, NPS or satisfaction target, sales funnel tracking, sponsor reporting, after-action review. |
| Sustainability and ethics | 6 | Waste/transport/vendor choices, community impact, inclusion, labor/vendor fairness, neighborhood disruption plan. |

## Score Bands

- 90-100: Flagship. Promote heavily, pursue sponsors/partners, capture case study data.
- 80-89: Strong. Publish after minor fixes; optimize marketing, accessibility, or measurement details.
- 70-79: Viable. Publish only after resolving named weaknesses and adding missing proof.
- 55-69: Needs rebuild. Keep internal; rewrite concept, business case, logistics, or audience strategy.
- 0-54: Reject or archive. Too generic, unsafe, economically weak, legally risky, or not credible.

## Universal Event Checklist

Every event record should answer these questions before publication:

1. Who is this for, and why would that audience choose this event now?
2. What is the central promise in one sentence?
3. What will attendees actually do minute by minute or act by act?
4. What makes the event distinct from a generic party, panel, show, or meetup?
5. What is the venue, date, time, capacity, age policy, and accessibility path?
6. Who produces, hosts, performs, judges, sponsors, or benefits from the event?
7. What are the hard costs, expected revenue, break-even attendance, and margin?
8. What are the safety, crowd, medical, security, weather, and cancellation plans?
9. What data will prove whether the event worked?
10. What follow-up turns one attendee into a repeat attendee, subscriber, buyer, donor, applicant, or community member?

## Business-Case Criteria

Assess business viability separately from creative quality. A beautiful concept can still fail if its economics or risk profile are weak.

| Business Factor | Required Evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Demand | Past attendance, waitlist, comparable market data, search/social signals, partner audience, or paid pre-sales. |
| Revenue | Ticket tiers, VIP/table packages, sponsor inventory, vendor fees, concessions, merch, memberships, donations, media rights, or lead generation value. |
| Costs | Venue, production, security, insurance, talent, staffing, permits, marketing, ticketing fees, contingency, cleanup, accessibility accommodations. |
| Break-even | Attendance and revenue required to cover fixed and variable costs. Include conservative, expected, and upside cases. |
| Sponsor fit | Sponsor audience match, deliverables, measurement plan, brand safety, renewal value. |
| Operational risk | Weather, safety, permitting, talent cancellation, low sales, neighborhood disruption, reputational risk, compliance. |
| Strategic value | Brand lift, community growth, destination impact, content capture, pipeline creation, partner relationships, repeatability. |

## Event-Type Criteria

### Conferences

Strong conferences are not just speaker lists. They need an audience problem, a curated agenda, networking design, sponsor ROI, and professional logistics.

- Require a clear attendee persona, job-to-be-done, speaker roster, session hosts/moderators, and facilitator assignments.
- Check speaker credibility, topic specificity, and session sequencing.
- Include networking formats beyond passive mingling.
- Verify sponsor/exhibitor value: qualified leads, meetings, demos, thought leadership, post-event reporting.
- Measure registration conversion, attendance rate, session satisfaction, lead quality, NPS, renewal intent, and pipeline influence.

### Concerts

Concerts need a clear artist bill, venue fit, production plan, audience draw, and ticketing strategy.

- Require headliner, supporting artists, DJs, hosts/MCs if any, set times, genre, age policy, and promoter/producer.
- Verify artist draw with streaming/social/audience evidence, prior ticket sales, local fanbase, or partner reach.
- Confirm production readiness: stage plot, sound, lighting, backline, hospitality, security, load-in/load-out, green room, settlement, and curfew.
- Model revenue through ticket tiers, VIP, tables, merch, sponsorships, bar share, and artist/promoter split.
- Measure tickets sold, sell-through by tier, check-in rate, bar/merch revenue, audience satisfaction, incident rate, artist/promoter renewal intent, and repeat-buyer capture.

### Circuit Parties And Nightlife Events

Circuit parties depend on trust, sound, venue flow, identity/community fit, safety, and credible talent.

- Verify DJ, host, entertainer, dancer, performer, or artist draw, production value, lighting/sound, door policy, age policy, and crowd capacity.
- Require harm-reduction, medical, security, harassment, and rideshare/transportation planning.
- Assess audience authenticity; generic LGBTQ+ or nightlife copy is not enough.
- Model revenue through tickets, VIP, tables, bar share, sponsorships, and repeat-promoter loyalty.
- Watch for risks: unsafe crowding, vague venue details, overpromising talent, exclusionary or exploitative positioning.

### Plays And Live Theater

Plays succeed through artistic clarity, casting/directing credibility, venue fit, production readiness, and audience development.

- Require title/rights status, playwright where relevant, director, producers, cast list, creative team, run dates, venue, runtime, content advisory, and ticket tiers.
- Assess why this production matters now: interpretation, community relevance, star/cast appeal, critical hook, school/group sales, or tourism draw.
- Confirm technical readiness: stage, lighting, sound, costumes, set, union/labor needs, load-in, rehearsals.
- Measure paid occupancy, reviews, audience satisfaction, group sales, donor conversion, and repeat attendance.

### Competitions, Including American Idol-Style Formats

Competitions need transparent rules and a credible advancement engine. American Idol's durability comes from an audition funnel, recognizable judges, contestant storytelling, public voting, weekly stakes, and a winner outcome.

- Require eligibility rules, application/audition process, judging rubric, advancement format, voting rules, prize, schedule, and dispute handling.
- Separate judge scoring from audience voting; explain how ties, saves, wildcards, eliminations, and disqualifications work.
- Evaluate contestant support: rehearsal, coaching, safety, releases, minors policy if applicable, and post-win opportunity.
- Build audience investment with contestant stories, theme weeks/rounds, live reveal moments, and shareable clips.
- Measure applications, audition completion, audience votes, retention between rounds, livestream/viewership, social engagement, sponsor lift, and winner outcomes.

### Festivals

Festivals are multi-system operations. The event must work as a lineup, a site plan, a vendor market, a safety plan, and a local economic story.

- Verify lineup/program density, music/artist lineup for music festivals, stage timing, hosts/MCs, entertainers, performers, vendors, site map, restroom/water/shade, ADA routes, transport, weather plan, and emergency plan.
- Model revenue from tickets, sponsorships, vendor fees, beverage/food share, merch, parking, grants, and tourism partners.
- Assess community impact: local business benefit, traffic/noise mitigation, public-space permits, cleanup, neighborhood communication.
- Measure attendance, dwell time, per-cap spending, vendor sales, incident rates, satisfaction, destination impact, and repeat intent.

## Talent And Credit Requirements

Events should store people and organizations as structured credits, not only as prose inside the description. Each credit should include a display name, role, sort order, optional bio, optional website/social link, and optional headshot or logo.

Recommended roles:

- Speaker
- Host
- MC
- Moderator
- Entertainer
- Performer
- Artist
- DJ
- Musician
- Band
- Cast
- Director
- Playwright
- Producer
- Judge
- Coach
- Vendor
- Sponsor

Required credits by type:

| Event Type | Required Credits |
| --- | --- |
| Conference | Speakers, hosts/moderators, organizers, sponsors if sold. |
| Concert | Headliner, supporting artists or bands, DJ/host where applicable, promoter/producer. |
| Circuit party/nightlife | DJs, hosts, entertainers, performers, producers, venue partner. |
| Play/theater | Cast, director, playwright where applicable, producer/company, key creative team. |
| Competition | Hosts, judges, contestants/teams when public, coaches/mentors if relevant, prize sponsor if any. |
| Festival | Artist/music lineup for music festivals, performers/entertainers, hosts/MCs, vendors, sponsors, producers. |

## Content Realism Standards

Every event should feel like a real listing created by a real organizer. Rewrite any event with these issues:

- Vague titles such as "Amazing Music Night" or "Business Conference 2026."
- No concrete date, time, venue, neighborhood, organizer, host, lineup, or format.
- Generic AI-style body copy that could apply to any city or audience.
- Price/capacity mismatch, such as a 5,000-person event in a small venue with no crowd plan.
- Missing age policy for nightlife, alcohol, adult content, competitions, or late-night events.
- Missing access details for ticketing, seating, routes, restrooms, service animals, or companion seating where applicable.
- No business case beyond "sell tickets."
- No post-event measurement plan.

## Admin Assessment Workflow

1. Export all existing event records from the admin account with id, title, type, date, venue, city, organizer, description, ticketing, capacity, price, speakers, hosts, entertainers, performers, artists, cast/lineup, sponsors, status, and metrics.
2. Normalize event types to: conference, concert, circuit party/nightlife, play/theater, competition, festival, workshop/class, market/fair, fundraiser, community, or other.
3. Score each event against the 100-point model.
4. Flag missing facts separately from weak concepts. Missing facts can be fixed quickly; weak concepts need product/business decisions.
5. Rewrite event copy only after the concept, logistics, and business case are coherent.
6. Mark each event as publish, publish after fixes, rebuild, archive, or reject.
7. Store the assessment with reviewer, date, score, recommendations, and source evidence.
8. Reassess after each event using actual performance data.
